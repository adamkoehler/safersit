Problems

Varicose Veins

Abnormal Veins that appear snakelike just below the skin that indicate underlying problems that are causing high venous blood pressure in the legs. The varicose veins are usually caused by a hereditary underlying valvular dysfunction of the Great Saphenous Vein or the Small Saphenous vein. The exact cause can be determined by Duplex Ultrasound.

Dermatitis

Many persons with venous circulation problems develop a type of skin condition, or dermatitis, known as Stasis Dermatitis. When the blood flow in veins is very slow or stagnant, blood starts to break down and release chemicals that cause inflammation and irritation. The condition is most pronounced in the lower leg and ankles and consists of dryness, flakiness, skin thinning, increased skin pigment, and large clusters of spider veins over the ankles. The skin can even become inflamed and red and break down to form ulcers that are very difficult to heal, so called Venostasis Ulcers.

Spider Veins

Fine branched red or purple surface veins that are often a cosmetic problems though most patients say their legs feel better after treatment. They are very common in women and there is a hereditary tendency. Some patients, however, do have underlying vein problems that can be determined by Duplex Ultrasound when necessary.

Venous Sores

Blood clots are usually associated with stagnant blood flow, injury to the blood vessel, and a condition occurring that makes blood more susceptible to clotting (or hypercoagulable state). So a patient lying in bed after surgery or an illness, or a person who has been hit in the leg in an accident, or someone sitting on a plane for a long period of time are at greater risk. The clots may be in surface(superficial) veins or the deep (intramuscular veins). Surface clots or superficial phlebitis is often in varicose veins and is usually not a life threatening situation. Deep Vein Thrombophlebitis (DVT) is not visable on the surface of the skin and these clots may travel to the heart and lung and be life-threatening. The only way to detect a deep vein clot is with Duplex Ultrasound.
